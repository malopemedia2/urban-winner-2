Mxolisi “The Great” Masilela

Mxolisi “The Great” Masilela often speaks with reverence about the force that shaped his life and became the foundation of his art.

I was raised by a remarkable traditional healer, a woman of profound wisdom, whose life was a tapestry woven from devotion, courage, and mystery. She believed in both God and magic, in the visible and the invisible, in the worlds of the living and the spirits who watch over us. Her hands healed bodies, yes, but they also healed souls. Her voice soothed, guided, and, at times, commanded. Her presence alone could still a room and make hearts beat in quiet awe.

Her home was not just a dwelling; it was a temple, a sanctuary where ancestors spoke and where dreams were guided with gentle but firm hands. Walls lined with sacred objects, shelves holding jars of herbs and potions, a small fire constantly burning to honor the spirits — to enter her home was to step into another dimension, a world where the ordinary and extraordinary coexisted seamlessly. Every corner held a story, every scent a memory, every sound a lesson.

From a young age, he learned that life was more than what could be seen with the eyes. He learned to listen to the wind, to interpret dreams, to feel the energy in a room, to honor the invisible threads that connect us to each other and to the past. He watched as she prayed for the sick, not merely reciting words, but summoning the courage, the faith, and the ancestral power to heal. And when the night fell, he would sit quietly by her side, listening to her recount tales of ancestors, spirits, and the sacred laws that govern the world.

She was both tender and fierce. She could lull a crying child to sleep with a whispered chant, and yet, when necessary, she could confront fear, doubt, and injustice with unshakable authority. In her, the divine and the human met, and she walked both paths with grace and power.

When she passed away, it felt as though the earth itself had shifted. The grief was a tidal wave, overwhelming and endless. But grief, for him, was not only sorrow — it was also awakening. It was a call to take the wisdom, the magic, and the sacred practices she had shared, and to carry them forward into the world. It was a summons to create, to honor, and to give life to her spirit in new forms.

This is why theatre became more than performance for Mxolisi — it became ritual, invocation, and offering. Each play he writes, each performance he directs, each stage he steps onto is an altar. The chants echo her voice, the rhythm of movements mirrors the cadence of her ceremonies, and the silences — oh, the silences — carry the weight of prayer and ancestral presence. Theatre, in his hands, is not merely entertainment; it is communion.

It was from this sacred well of memory and devotion that Leina – The Masterpiece emerged. Leina was born not from a desire to impress, not from ambition, but from love, grief, and remembrance. Every scene, every line, every pause was infused with the rhythm of her rituals, the energy of her prayers, and the weight of her presence. The audience did not just watch Leina — they felt it. They were transported into a world where the spiritual and the theatrical intertwined seamlessly, where sorrow could be sacred, and joy could be transcendent.

The work was met with acclaim. Leina – The Masterpiece received the Silver Ovation Award at the prestigious National Arts Festival, presented by Standard Bank as part of the celebrated Standard Bank Ovation Awards.

Through Leina, Mxolisi carries forward a living conversation with his mentor. Every actor who steps on the stage, every drumbeat, every breath, every silence is a tribute. Her teachings — of faith, of magic, of balance between worlds — are not only preserved but breathed anew into the hearts of those who experience them.

Every time the curtains rise, he does not only perform — he invokes. Every applause is not only recognition — it is a prayer answered. And every tear shed in the audience is not only emotion — it is remembrance. Her spirit leads. Her teachings remain. And through his art, she lives on — not only remembered but celebrated, honored, and reborn in every heartbeat of the theatre.
